Apology

Ἀπολογία Σωκράτους

Date: c. 399–395 BCE Setting: The law court of Athens Characters: Socrates, Meletus (accuser)

The Apology is Plato's version of the speech Socrates delivered at his trial in 399 BCE. It is not an apology in the modern sense — the Greek word apologia means a defence speech. Socrates stands accused of impiety (asebeia) and corrupting the youth of Athens. His defence is a defiant and uncompromising statement of his philosophical mission.

Socrates begins by distinguishing himself from the earlier natural philosophers and the Sophists, disclaiming any knowledge of the subjects they teach. He then recounts the story of the Delphic Oracle, which declared that no one was wiser than Socrates. Puzzled by this, he set out to refute the oracle by finding someone wiser, only to discover that those with reputations for wisdom — politicians, poets, craftsmen — did not truly know what they claimed to know.

Socrates concludes that he is wisest only in this: he knows that he does not know. This realization has made him a social gadfly, sent by the god to sting Athens into examining its own beliefs about virtue, justice, and the good life. His philosophical questioning has earned him powerful enemies, but he refuses to stop, declaring: "The unexamined life is not worth living."

After being found guilty, Socrates proposes ironically that he be rewarded with free meals in the Prytaneum rather than punished. When the death penalty is imposed, he addresses the court with remarkable calm, predicting that his execution will bring greater shame upon his accusers than upon himself. He ends with the assurance that no harm can come to a good man in life or death.

Key Themes

  • Philosophical humility and Socratic ignorance
  • The examined life as the only life worth living
  • The role of the philosopher in society
  • Integrity in the face of death
  • The tension between philosophy and politics

Historical Context

The trial of Socrates took place in a politically charged atmosphere. Athens had recently lost the Peloponnesian War and endured the brutal rule of the Thirty Tyrants — several of whom had been Socrates' associates. Although amnesty had been declared, the charges against Socrates carried political overtones. Plato's Apology is both a historical document and a literary masterpiece, shaping the figure of Socrates for all subsequent philosophy.
