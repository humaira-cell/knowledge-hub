Cratylus

Κρατύλος

Date: c. 390–380 BCE Setting: Athens Characters: Socrates, Cratylus, Hermogenes

The Cratylus is Plato's pioneering contribution to the philosophy of language. It investigates a question that remains central to linguistics: is the relationship between words and their meanings natural or conventional? Hermogenes argues that names are purely conventional — whatever we agree to call something is its correct name. Cratylus, by contrast, maintains that names have a natural correctness, reflecting the true nature of the things they signify.

Socrates mediates the debate, initially appearing to side with Cratylus's naturalism. He engages in an extended and often playful analysis of Greek etymologies, tracing the origins of words for gods, heavenly bodies, virtues, and fundamental concepts. These etymologies, while often fanciful by modern standards, reveal Plato's belief that language encodes deep truths about reality — that names are tools that reveal the nature of things.

However, Socrates also raises serious objections to extreme naturalism. If names perfectly revealed the essence of things, then the study of language alone would give us knowledge of reality — but Socrates doubts this. Language may be an imperfect instrument, and the names given by the first name-givers may reflect their own (possibly mistaken) beliefs about the world.

The dialogue concludes with a compromise. Names have a natural dimension — they are not purely arbitrary — but language is also conventional and imperfect. The most reliable path to truth is not through the analysis of words but through the direct investigation of reality itself, which is known through the Forms. The Cratylus thus subordinates the study of language to metaphysics and epistemology, anticipating the Platonic theory that true knowledge is of the Forms, not of words.

Key Themes

  • Natural vs. conventional theories of language
  • Etymology as a path to understanding
  • Names as tools for instruction and distinguishing
  • The limitations of language as a guide to truth
  • The priority of Forms over words

Historical Context

The historical Cratylus was a philosopher of the Heraclitean school who held that everything is in flux so radical that even naming is impossible — he would merely wag his finger. According to Aristotle, the young Plato was first introduced to philosophy through Cratylus. The dialogue thus has a personal dimension, representing Plato's engagement with his own intellectual heritage and his movement beyond Heraclitean relativism.
