Crito

Κρίτων

Date: c. 399–395 BCE Setting: Socrates' prison cell, Athens Characters: Socrates, Crito

The Crito is a short but powerful dialogue set in Socrates' prison cell shortly before his execution. His wealthy friend Crito has arranged a plan for Socrates to escape from Athens and live in exile. The dialogue takes the form of a single dramatic question: should Socrates escape or accept his unjust punishment?

Crito presents several practical arguments: Socrates' death would rob his friends of a beloved companion and damage their reputation, as people would think they were too cowardly or too poor to save him. Moreover, Socrates would be abandoning his children and his philosophical mission. Crito urges him to act while there is still time.

Socrates responds not by appealing to emotion but by subjecting Crito's arguments to rational scrutiny. The key question, he insists, is not what people will think but what is just. It is never right to return injustice for injustice. Breaking the law — even an unjust law — would harm the very foundation of the city that nurtured him.

Socrates then famously personifies the Laws of Athens, who argue in a speech that they have given him birth, education, and every benefit of civilization. He has had seventy years to leave if he found them unsatisfactory. By remaining in Athens, he has tacitly agreed to obey them. To break the laws now would be to destroy the city and betray his own principles. The Crito thus presents one of the earliest and most influential arguments for the social contract theory of political obligation.

Key Themes

  • Civic duty and the social contract
  • The obligation to obey the law
  • Never returning injustice for injustice
  • The priority of principle over public opinion
  • Integrity and consistency in moral action

Historical Context

Along with the Apology and the Phaedo, the Crito forms part of Plato's trilogy on the trial and death of Socrates. While the Apology presents Socrates' public defence and the Phaedo his final philosophical discussion, the Crito captures the private moment of decision between Socrates and his closest friend. The dialogue reflects the real historical possibility of escape and the weight of Socrates' actual choice.
