Euthyphro

Εὐθύφρων

Date: c. 399–395 BCE Setting: The Stoa Basileios, Athens courthouse Characters: Socrates, Euthyphro

The Euthyphro is one of Plato's early dialogues, set in the months before Socrates' trial. Socrates encounters Euthyphro — a religious prophet who claims special knowledge of divine matters — at the courthouse where Euthyphro is prosecuting his own father for murder. The dialogue is a classic example of the Socratic elenchus, in which the pretensions of someone who claims expertise are systematically dismantled.

Socrates, who has been indicted for impiety (asebeia), expresses interest in learning from Euthyphro what piety is. Euthyphro confidently offers a series of definitions, each of which Socrates subjects to critical examination. Euthyphro's first definition — piety is prosecuting wrongdoers, as he is doing — is too narrow. His second — piety is what is loved by the gods — leads to the famous Euthyphro dilemma: is something pious because the gods love it, or do the gods love it because it is pious?

The Euthyphro dilemma has profound implications for the relationship between morality and religion. If the gods love the pious because it is already pious, then piety is independent of divine will and the gods are not the source of morality. If something is pious only because the gods love it, then morality is arbitrary and could change if the gods changed their minds.

Euthyphro continues to offer definitions — piety as a part of justice, piety as service to the gods — but each proves inadequate. Eventually, Euthyphro hurries away on his own business, leaving the question unanswered. The dialogue is a masterpiece of dramatic irony: the man who claims expert knowledge of piety cannot define it, while the man accused of impiety is the one who truly examines the question.

Key Themes

  • The definition of piety (to hosion)
  • The Euthyphro dilemma and divine command theory
  • Socratic ignorance vs. pretended knowledge
  • The relationship between justice and piety
  • Impiety as a legal and philosophical charge

Historical Context

The Euthyphro takes place in early 399 BCE, just before Socrates' trial. Meletus, Socrates' accuser, is said to be somewhere in the courthouse. The dialogue thus serves as a dramatic prelude to the Apology, exploring the concept of piety that is at the heart of the charges against Socrates. The historical Euthyphro is otherwise unknown, but his confidence in his own religious knowledge makes him a perfect foil for Socrates.
