Gorgias

Γοργίας

Date: c. 380 BCE Setting: Athens, perhaps at the home of Callicles Characters: Socrates, Gorgias, Polus, Callicles

The Gorgias is one of Plato's most passionate and combative dialogues, a fierce critique of rhetoric and the political culture of democratic Athens. Socrates confronts three interlocutors in succession — the famous rhetorician Gorgias, his young follower Polus, and the cynical aristocrat Callicles — each representing a more extreme version of the view that might makes right.

The dialogue begins with Socrates asking Gorgias to define rhetoric. Gorgias boasts that rhetoric is the greatest art, giving its practitioner the power to persuade any audience and win any argument. Socrates retorts that rhetoric is not an art at all but a "knack" — a form of flattery like cookery or cosmetics, concerned with producing pleasure rather than truth. It is a counterfeit of justice, just as cosmetics is a counterfeit of gymnastic.

Polus, outraged, argues that orators and tyrants are the most powerful people in the world. Socrates famously responds with one of his most radical claims: it is worse to commit injustice than to suffer it, and the worst fate that can befall a person is to do wrong and escape punishment. The unjust person is like a sick person who refuses to see a doctor — punishment is the medicine that heals the soul.

Callicles then enters the fray, defending the natural right of the strong to rule the weak. He accuses Socrates of childish philosophy that leaves a man defenceless in the real world. Socrates replies with a vision of the philosophical life as the only truly good life, governed by order and self-discipline rather than the endless pursuit of pleasure. The dialogue ends with an eschatological myth picturing the judgment of souls in the afterlife.

Key Themes

  • The critique of rhetoric as flattery, not art
  • It is better to suffer than to commit injustice
  • Punishment as medicine for the soul
  • The refutation of Callicles' might-makes-right morality
  • Order and self-discipline vs. the unbridled pursuit of pleasure

Historical Context

The Gorgias was written in the wake of the Peloponnesian War, when Athenian democracy was turning toward demagoguery and imperial aggression. The character of Callicles may represent a real historical figure or a type — the ambitious young aristocrat who believes that conventional morality is merely a tool used by the weak to restrain the strong. The dialogue stands as Plato's most powerful statement of the priority of justice over power.
