Laches

Λάχης

Date: c. 395–380 BCE Setting: Athens, a public gathering place Characters: Socrates, Laches, Nicias, Lysimachus, Melesias

The Laches is an early Socratic dialogue that examines the nature of courage. Two fathers, Lysimachus and Melesias, are concerned about the proper education of their sons and seek advice from two famous Athenian generals, Laches and Nicias. They also invite Socrates, whose reputation for wisdom has reached them, to join the discussion.

The conversation begins with the question of whether the boys should be trained in fighting in armour — the latest military fashion. But Socrates quickly redirects the discussion to the more fundamental question: what is courage? For how can one decide whether fighting in armour produces courage if one does not know what courage is?

Laches, a blunt and courageous soldier, offers the first definition: courage is standing firm and not running away in battle. Socrates objects that courage can be shown in many contexts — in illness, at sea, in politics — and that a mere definition by example does not capture the essence of courage. Laches then offers a second definition: courage is endurance of the soul. But Socrates points out that foolish endurance is not admirable, so courage must involve wisdom.

Nicias, the more intellectual general, offers a definition informed by his acquaintance with Socratic thinking: courage is knowledge of what is to be dreaded and what is not, both in war and in all circumstances. Socrates subjects this definition to careful examination, arguing that such knowledge would encompass all good and evil, making courage equivalent to virtue as a whole. The dialogue ends in aporia, with the definition of courage still unresolved — but the examination itself has demonstrated the courage of philosophical inquiry.

Key Themes

  • The definition of courage (andreia)
  • Whether courage is a part of virtue or virtue as a whole
  • Courage as knowledge of what is and is not to be feared
  • Education of the young and the problem of moral expertise
  • The unity of the virtues

Historical Context

The dialogue is named after the historical Laches, an Athenian general who died in the Battle of Mantinea in 418 BCE. Nicias, the other general, was a leading figure in Athens who met his death in the disastrous Sicilian Expedition of 413 BCE. The dramatic date of the dialogue is thus before 418 BCE, giving it an air of tragic irony — both generals who discuss courage would eventually die in battle.
