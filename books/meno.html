Meno

Μένων

Date: c. 395–380 BCE Setting: Athens, perhaps in the Agora Characters: Socrates, Meno, Anytus, one of Meno's slaves

The Meno is a transitional dialogue in which Plato begins to move beyond the purely negative, aporetic style of the early Socratic dialogues toward a more constructive philosophical method. The central question is whether virtue can be taught — a question of urgent practical importance in democratic Athens, where anyone could aspire to political leadership.

The dialogue opens with Meno asking Socrates whether virtue is acquired by teaching, by practice, or by nature. Socrates characteristically replies that he cannot answer because he does not know what virtue is. Meno offers several definitions, but Socrates refutes each one, reducing Meno to a state of confusion — the famous experience of aporia.

Meno then poses a devastating challenge: if you do not know what something is, how can you search for it? How will you recognize it when you find it? This paradox of inquiry is one of the deepest epistemological problems in philosophy. Socrates responds with the theory of recollection (anamnesis): learning is not the acquisition of new information but the recovery of knowledge the soul already possesses from a previous existence. He demonstrates this by leading an uneducated slave boy to discover a geometric theorem through questioning alone.

The dialogue ultimately reaches a nuanced conclusion: virtue is not teachable in the ordinary sense, because there are no teachers of virtue. True virtue is a kind of true opinion that comes by divine dispensation — a gift of the gods rather than a subject that can be transmitted by instruction. This conclusion leaves room for Socratic philosophizing as the practice that can turn true opinion into knowledge.

Key Themes

  • Whether virtue can be taught
  • The paradox of inquiry and the theory of recollection
  • Socratic method and the elenchus
  • Knowledge vs. true opinion
  • The nature of virtue itself

Historical Context

The Meno is set around 402 BCE, a few years before Socrates' trial. Anytus, who appears as a character in the dialogue, would later be one of Socrates' accusers. The dialogue thus foreshadows the conflicts that would lead to Socrates' death. The demonstration with the slave boy was revolutionary in its implication that knowledge is innate — an idea that would influence rationalist philosophers from Augustine to Leibniz and Chomsky.
