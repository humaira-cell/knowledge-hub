Parmenides

Παρμενίδης

Date: c. 365–360 BCE Setting: Athens, perhaps in the Kerameikos Characters: Parmenides, Zeno, Socrates (young), Aristoteles (later one of the Thirty)

The Parmenides is perhaps Plato's most challenging and enigmatic dialogue. It features a young Socrates — probably in his early twenties — confronting the great Eleatic philosopher Parmenides and his student Zeno. The dialogue is traditionally divided into two parts: a critique of the Theory of Forms and an astonishing series of dialectical exercises on the nature of the One.

In the first part, Parmenides subjects the Theory of Forms to a devastating critique. He poses several famous problems: What things have Forms? Are there Forms of hair, mud, and dirt? If particulars participate in Forms, do they participate in the whole Form or a part? The "Third Man" argument shows that if large things are large by participating in the Form of Largeness, then the Form itself must participate in a further Form of Largeness, leading to an infinite regress.

Young Socrates offers various defences, but Parmenides shows each one to be inadequate. He concludes that if Socrates is to master philosophy, he must undergo a rigorous training in dialectic — the very exercise that Parmenides himself then demonstrates in the second part of the dialogue.

The second part consists of eight deductions (or hypotheses) concerning the nature of the One. If the One is, what follows? If the One is not, what follows? Each deduction draws out seemingly contradictory conclusions with relentless logical precision. The Parmenides has been read as a critique of his own earlier theory, as a masterpiece of logical exercise, and as a profound metaphysical exploration of the relationship between unity and multiplicity.

Key Themes

  • Self-critique of the Theory of Forms
  • The Third Man argument and infinite regress
  • Participation and the relationship of Forms to particulars
  • Dialectical method as philosophical training
  • The One and the Many: logical deductions

Historical Context

The dramatic date of the Parmenides is about 450 BCE, when the historical Parmenides and Zeno visited Athens and met the young Socrates. This is chronologically impossible — Socrates would have been around twenty, and Parmenides about sixty-five. The dialogue is thus consciously fictional, using the meeting as a literary device to stage a confrontation between Eleatic monism and Platonic metaphysics.
