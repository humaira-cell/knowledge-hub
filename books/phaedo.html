Phaedo

Φαίδων

Date: c. 360–355 BCE Setting: Socrates' prison cell, Athens Characters: Socrates, Phaedo, Simmias, Cebes, Crito, Phaedo of Elis (narrator)

The Phaedo is Plato's dramatic account of Socrates' final hours before his execution by hemlock. Narrated by Phaedo to the Pythagorean philosopher Echecrates, the dialogue captures Socrates' serene and reasoned discussion of the immortality of the soul on the last day of his life. It is at once a philosophical argument, a literary masterpiece, and a profound meditation on death and the meaning of philosophy.

Socrates presents four main arguments for the soul's immortality. The Argument from Opposites holds that life arises from death as death arises from life, implying a cyclical process of rebirth. The Theory of Recollection argues that learning is a process of remembering knowledge the soul possessed before birth, proving its pre-existence. The Argument from Affinity distinguishes the invisible, immortal soul from the visible, mortal body. The final and most complex argument — the Argument from Forms — demonstrates that the soul, as the principle of life, cannot admit death.

The dialogue also contains Socrates' famous critique of misology — hatred of argument — warning against the temptation to abandon rational inquiry when arguments seem to fail. He urges his followers to continue seeking truth even in the face of apparent contradiction.

The Phaedo concludes with Socrates' death scene, one of the most moving passages in all of Western literature. After bidding farewell to his weeping friends, he drinks the hemlock calmly, walks around until his legs grow heavy, and lies down to die. His last words ask Crito to offer a sacrifice to Asclepius — perhaps thanking the god of healing for the cure of death.

Key Themes

  • The immortality of the soul
  • Philosophy as preparation for death
  • The theory of recollection and pre-existence
  • The separation of soul from body
  • Reincarnation and the cycle of births

Historical Context

Socrates was executed in 399 BCE after being convicted of impiety and corrupting the youth. Plato was not present at the death — he mentions his own absence due to illness — and the Phaedo may represent both a tribute to his teacher and a defense of Socrates' philosophical legacy. The Pythagorean setting (Phaedo narrates to a Pythagorean) reflects the influence of Pythagorean thought on Plato's conception of the soul.
