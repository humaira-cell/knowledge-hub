Phaedrus

Φαῖδρος

Date: c. 370–360 BCE Setting: A countryside path outside Athens Characters: Socrates, Phaedrus

The Phaedrus is one of Plato's most beautiful and wide-ranging dialogues, set in a pastoral scene outside Athens where Socrates and his young friend Phaedrus walk along the Ilissus river. It opens with Phaedrus reciting a speech by the orator Lysias arguing that a non-lover should be favoured over a lover. This provokes Socrates into delivering two speeches of his own — one ironic and one sublime — and the dialogue unfolds into a profound discussion of love, rhetoric, the soul, and writing itself.

Socrates' first speech, delivered with his head covered in shame, mimics and criticizes Lysias's argument. His second speech — the great palinode or recantation — is one of the high points of Platonic literature. In it, Socrates describes the soul as a winged chariot drawn by two horses: one noble and obedient, the other unruly and base. The soul's wings enable it to ascend to the realm of the Forms, but when the wings are lost, the soul falls to earth and incarnates in a human body.

The madness of love, Socrates argues, is actually a divine gift — the greatest of blessings. When we see beauty in another person, our wings begin to grow again, and we are inspired to recollect the Form of Beauty itself. The true lover's response to beauty leads to philosophy, not mere sensual gratification. The Phaedrus thus presents a vision of erotic love as the beginning of the soul's ascent to the divine.

The second half of the dialogue turns to rhetoric and the nature of persuasive speech. Socrates argues that true rhetoric must be based on knowledge of truth and the nature of the soul. He also offers a famous critique of writing, warning that written words cannot answer questions or defend themselves — they are like a painted portrait that appears alive but cannot speak. This critique is ironically delivered in a written dialogue, adding a layer of self-consciousness to Plato's own literary enterprise.

Key Themes

  • The chariot allegory of the soul
  • Love as divine madness and philosophical inspiration
  • Recollection of the Forms through beauty
  • The critique of writing and the limits of text
  • True rhetoric as psychagogy — leading the soul

Historical Context

The Phaedrus is often considered a late middle or early late dialogue, reflecting Plato's mature thought on the relationship between philosophy, love, and language. The critique of writing is particularly striking given that Plato himself wrote dialogues — it may represent a recognition of the limitations of the written form and a reminder that philosophy ultimately requires living conversation.
