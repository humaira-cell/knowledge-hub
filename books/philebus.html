Philebus

Φίληβος

Date: c. 360–355 BCE Setting: Athens Characters: Socrates, Protarchus, Philebus

The Philebus is a late dialogue examining one of the central questions of ancient ethics: what is the good life for human beings? The dialogue stages a debate between pleasure and wisdom, with Philebus defending pleasure as the highest good and Socrates arguing for the supremacy of knowledge. Protarchus takes over for Philebus early on, and the remainder of the dialogue is a sophisticated exploration of the nature of both pleasure and knowledge.

Socrates argues that the good life cannot consist of pleasure alone — a life of pure pleasure without any intelligence or memory would not be a human life worth living. Nor can it consist of wisdom alone — knowledge without any satisfaction or enjoyment would be incomplete. The good life must be a mixture of both, but a mixture requires knowledge of the right measure and proportion.

The dialogue contains Plato's most detailed analysis of pleasure: its kinds, its purity, and its relationship to truth. Socrates distinguishes between true and false pleasures, pure and mixed pleasures, and pleasures of the body versus pleasures of the soul. He argues that intellectual pleasures — the pleasures of learning, understanding, and contemplating — are superior because they are more pure and less dependent on the removal of pain.

The Philebus also introduces the important metaphysical framework of the four classes: the unlimited, the limited, the mixture of both, and the cause of the mixture. This ontological scheme is used to analyze both the cosmos and the human good, showing that the good life, like the universe itself, is a harmonious mixture of limit and limitlessness, order and variety.

Key Themes

  • Pleasure vs. wisdom as the highest good
  • The mixed life as the best life
  • True and false pleasures
  • The fourfold classification of being
  • Measure, proportion, and beauty as marks of the good

Historical Context

The Philebus is one of Plato's last dialogues, likely written after the Sophist and Statesman. It shows the influence of Pythagorean mathematics on Plato's later thought, particularly in the use of limit and the unlimited as fundamental principles. The dialogue's nuanced treatment of pleasure represents a more balanced view than the stark rejection of the body in the Phaedo.
