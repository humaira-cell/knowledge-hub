Protagoras

Πρωταγόρας

Date: c. 390–380 BCE Setting: The house of Callias, Athens Characters: Socrates, Protagoras, Hippias, Prodicus, Alcibiades, Callias

The Protagoras is a lively and dramatic dialogue in which Socrates debates the most famous sophist of the age on whether virtue can be taught. The setting is the home of Callias, where the great sophist Protagoras is staying during his visit to Athens, surrounded by a crowd of admirers. Socrates arrives with a young friend who wants to become a student of Protagoras, and the debate begins.

Protagoras argues that virtue can indeed be taught and delivers the Great Speech (the "Great Myth") explaining how Zeus gave justice and shame to all humans so that cities could exist. He claims to teach the art of citizenship — how to manage one's household and the city's affairs. Socrates questions whether virtue is really a teachable technê like architecture or medicine, or whether it is something that cannot be systematically transmitted.

The dialogue is famous for the detailed examination of the unity of virtue. Socrates presses Protagoras to admit that the various virtues — wisdom, courage, temperance, justice, and piety — are fundamentally one. Protagoras resists, but Socrates leads him through a series of arguments suggesting that all virtues are forms of knowledge. If virtue is knowledge, then no one does wrong willingly — the famous Socratic paradox.

The Protagoras is also noteworthy for its sophisticated literary structure. The entire dialogue is framed as a conversation Socrates recounts to a friend, and it contains numerous embedded speeches, playful mythological interpretations, and a brilliant analysis of a poem by Simonides. It ends with a curious reversal: Socrates began by arguing that virtue cannot be taught, but Protagoras's position forces him to argue that virtue is knowledge — which would make it teachable after all.

Key Themes

  • The teachability of virtue
  • The unity of the virtues
  • Knowledge as sufficient for virtue
  • Sophistry vs. philosophy
  • Hedonism and moral calculation

Historical Context

The dialogue is set around 433 BCE, before the Peloponnesian War, when Athens was at the height of its intellectual ferment. Protagoras was indeed the first and most famous sophist, known for the dictum "Man is the measure of all things." The gathering of sophists in Callias's house reflects the real atmosphere of intellectual competition in fifth-century Athens.
