Sophist

Σοφιστής

Date: c. 360–355 BCE Setting: A gymnasium in Athens Characters: The Eleatic Stranger, Theaetetus, Socrates (silent)

The Sophist is a late dialogue in which Plato, through the mouth of an Eleatic Stranger, undertakes a rigorous definition of the sophist — a figure of great importance and ambiguity in Athenian intellectual life. The dialogue is the companion piece to the Statesman and was originally intended to be followed by a dialogue on the philosopher, which was never written.

The dialogue employs the method of collection and division, a systematic technique of defining concepts by dividing genera into species. Through a series of seven definitions, the Eleatic Stranger attempts to capture the essence of the sophist. Each definition peels away another layer: the sophist is a hunter of young men, a merchant of knowledge, a combatant in eristic disputation, and ultimately a producer of illusions — a kind of magician who makes false statements appear true.

This last definition leads to a profound metaphysical problem: how can false statements exist at all? If to speak is to say something about what is, then false speech — speaking about what is not — seems impossible. The Sophist thus confronts the famous Parmenidean prohibition against speaking of non-being. The Eleatic Stranger argues that non-being must exist in some sense, as difference, not as the opposite of being.

The Sophist is one of Plato's most important contributions to ontology and philosophy of language. The analysis of the interweaving of Forms and the distinction between being and non-being as difference laid the groundwork for later developments in logic and metaphysics. The silent presence of Socrates throughout the dialogue symbolizes the passing of the torch from the Socratic to the Platonic method.

Key Themes

  • The definition of the sophist through division
  • Being and non-being: parricide of Parmenides
  • The nature of falsehood and illusion
  • The interweaving of Forms
  • The dialectical method of collection and division

Historical Context

The Sophist is set on the same day as the Theaetetus, just before Socrates' trial. The replacement of Socrates by the Eleatic Stranger as the main speaker signals a shift in method and doctrine in Plato's late period. The dialogue engages critically with the Eleatic school of Parmenides and Zeno, as well as with contemporary sophistry, to articulate a mature Platonic ontology.
