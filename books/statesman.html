Statesman

Πολιτικός

Date: c. 360–355 BCE Setting: A gymnasium in Athens Characters: The Eleatic Stranger, Young Socrates, Theodorus

The Statesman is the second dialogue in Plato's trilogy on the philosopher, the statesman, and the sophist. Following the Sophist, it continues the method of collection and division to define the statesman — the true political leader who possesses the art of ruling. The dialogue is led by the Eleatic Stranger, with Young Socrates (a namesake of the famous philosopher) as his respondent.

The definition of the statesman proceeds through a series of divisions that gradually narrow the field. The Stranger divides all knowledge into theoretical and practical, then into directive and productive, ultimately locating the statesman as a director of living beings who rules over willing subjects. A myth about the reversal of the cosmos — the Age of Cronos and the Age of Zeus — is introduced to illustrate the difference between divine guidance and human self-governance.

Plato argues that the true statesman possesses the royal art of weaving together the different elements of society: the courageous and the moderate, the active and the contemplative. Unlike the "constitutional kings" who rule by law, the true statesman rules by knowledge itself. However, recognizing that such ideal rulers are rare, the dialogue also discusses the role of law as a necessary surrogate, comparing it to the instructions a doctor leaves for an assistant.

The Statesman refines the political theory of the Republic by introducing a more sophisticated classification of constitutions and by acknowledging the legitimacy of law in the absence of the ideal ruler. It represents Plato's continuing effort to define the relationship between philosophical knowledge and practical politics.

Key Themes

  • The definition of the statesman by division
  • The myth of the reversed cosmos and human autonomy
  • Knowledge as the true basis of rule
  • The art of political weaving: unity in diversity
  • The relationship between law and expert judgment

Historical Context

The Statesman continues the series that begins with the Theaetetus and Sophist, set on the same dramatic day. The choice of an Eleatic Stranger rather than Socrates as the main speaker allows Plato to develop ideas that may go beyond — or even modify — positions associated with the historical Socrates. The dialogue's nuanced treatment of law may reflect Plato's later experiences with political reform in Syracuse.
