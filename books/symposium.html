The Symposium

Συμπόσιον

Date: c. 385–370 BCE Setting: Agathon's house, Athens Characters: Socrates, Agathon, Aristophanes, Alcibiades, Pausanias, Eryximachus, Phaedrus

The Symposium is one of Plato's most literary and dramatic dialogues, recounting a drinking party at the home of the tragic poet Agathon. Each guest delivers a speech in praise of Eros (love), and the dialogue builds through these increasingly profound reflections toward the climactic speech of Socrates, who recounts the teachings of the priestess Diotima.

The speeches range from the playful to the philosophical. Phaedrus celebrates love's power to inspire noble deeds; Pausanias distinguishes between Common and Heavenly love; Eryximachus, a physician, sees love as a cosmic force of harmony; Aristophanes offers a comic myth of original human wholeness; and Agathon praises the beauty and virtue of love itself.

Socrates then transforms the discussion by relating what Diotima taught him: love is not a god but a daimon, a spirit that mediates between human and divine. Love is the desire for the perpetual possession of the good, and it drives a ascent from physical beauty to the beauty of souls, to the beauty of knowledge, and ultimately to the Form of Beauty itself — the famous "ladder of love."

The dialogue ends dramatically with the drunken entrance of Alcibiades, who delivers a passionate encomium to Socrates himself, praising his virtue, wisdom, and strange power to inspire love. The Symposium is thus not only a discourse on love but a living demonstration of the philosopher's erotic nature.

Key Themes

  • The nature of love (Eros) as desire for the good
  • The ascent from physical to spiritual beauty
  • The Form of Beauty as the ultimate object of love
  • Love as a daimonic intermediary between human and divine
  • The relationship between philosophy and erotic desire

Historical Context

The Symposium is set in 416 BCE, just before the disastrous Sicilian Expedition that would help destroy Athens. This dramatic date gives the dialogue a poignant undertone — the brilliant young Alcibiades, who bursts in drunk at the end, would soon be condemned for sacrilege and flee to Sparta. The dialogue showcases Plato's literary genius in weaving philosophy with drama, character, and historical allusion.
