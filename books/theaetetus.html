Theaetetus

Θεαίτητος

Date: c. 369–367 BCE Setting: A gymnasium in Athens Characters: Socrates, Theaetetus, Theodorus of Cyrene

The Theaetetus is Plato's most sustained and penetrating investigation of the nature of knowledge. The dialogue examines three definitions of knowledge in turn: knowledge as perception, knowledge as true judgment, and knowledge as true judgment with an account (logos). Each definition is explored with extraordinary subtlety before being found inadequate.

The dialogue opens with a charming scene: Socrates encounters the young Theaetetus, a gifted mathematician whose face resembles Socrates' own snub nose and bulging eyes. Theodorus, their mutual friend, praises Theaetetus's intelligence and character, and Socrates engages him in conversation about knowledge. Socrates presents himself as a midwife of ideas, helping others give birth to thoughts and testing whether they are viable.

The first definition — knowledge is perception — is examined through the lens of Protagoras's doctrine that "man is the measure of all things" and Heraclitus's theory of universal flux. If all is change and perception is relative, then knowledge becomes impossible. Socrates argues that perception is not knowledge because it tells us only about qualities, not about being or truth.

The second definition — knowledge as true judgment — is refuted by the example of a jury. The jury may form a true judgment based on persuasive testimony, but they do not thereby acquire knowledge, because they lack direct access to the facts. The third definition — true judgment with an account — is examined and found equally problematic. What could an "account" add that would turn true judgment into knowledge? The dialogue ends in aporia, with Socrates leaving to face his trial, and Theaetetus enriched despite reaching no conclusion.

Key Themes

  • The definition of knowledge (episteme)
  • Perception vs. knowledge
  • The refutation of Protagorean relativism
  • Knowledge as true judgment with an account
  • Socratic midwifery (maieutics)

Historical Context

The Theaetetus is generally considered an early middle-period dialogue, written about the time of Plato's first visit to Syracuse. It is set in 399 BCE, just before Socrates' trial — the same day, in fact, as the Euthyphro. The historical Theaetetus was a real mathematician who died fighting for Athens in 369 BCE, and the dialogue may have been written as a tribute to him.
