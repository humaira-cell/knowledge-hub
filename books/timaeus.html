Timaeus

Τίμαιος

Date: c. 360–355 BCE Setting: Socrates' house, Athens Characters: Timaeus of Locri, Socrates, Critias, Hermocrates

The Timaeus is Plato's ambitious cosmological dialogue, presenting an account of the creation of the universe by a divine craftsman or Demiurge. It was Plato's most influential work in the ancient and medieval periods — the only Platonic dialogue widely available in Latin throughout the Middle Ages — and shaped Western cosmology for nearly two thousand years.

The dialogue begins with a summary of the ideal city from the Republic, then transitions into Critias's account of ancient Athens and the lost island of Atlantis. The main speaker, Timaeus of Locri, a Pythagorean philosopher, then delivers a long and intricate speech describing the origin of the cosmos.

Timaeus argues that the universe was created by a good and benevolent Demiurge who looked to the eternal Forms as a model. Because the Demiurge is good, he desired that all things should be as good as possible. The physical world is therefore a "moving image of eternity" — time itself is created as the moving image of the timeless Forms. The world is a living being with a soul, and the circular motions of the heavens reflect the rational order of the World Soul.

The dialogue also contains detailed — if speculative — accounts of the four elements, the structure of the human body, sensation, and disease. Plato's physics is teleological: every natural phenomenon is explained in terms of its purpose and its contribution to the overall goodness of the cosmos. The Timaeus is thus not only a scientific treatise but a theological statement about the rationality and beauty of creation.

Key Themes

  • The Demiurge and the creation of the cosmos
  • The world as a living being with a soul
  • Time as the moving image of eternity
  • The four elements and geometrical atomism
  • Teleological explanation in natural science

Historical Context

The Timaeus was probably written after Plato's first visit to Syracuse, where he encountered Pythagorean philosophy. The dialogue's influence extends through Neoplatonism, Christian theology (which saw the Demiurge as a prefiguration of the Creator), and Renaissance science. Copernicus, Kepler, and Galileo all engaged with the Timaeus, and its vision of a mathematically ordered cosmos anticipated the scientific revolution.
